## Station-by-Station Answer Key

### Station 1 — Housing & Rent

| Choice | Monthly Cost | 30% Rule Check (at lowest salary: $2,005 net) |
|---|---|---|
| Live with parents | $200 | 10% — well within budget |
| Shared room / house | $650 | 32% — slightly over the 30% rule |
| Studio apartment | $850 | 42% — significantly over for lower salaries |
| 1-bedroom solo | $1,200 | 60% — unworkable on any salary in this simulation |

**Teaching note:** Students with salaries under $33,000/year who choose solo housing will almost certainly finish in deficit. This is intentional — it mirrors a real economic reality.

---

### Station 2 — Transportation

| Choice | Monthly Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bike + rideshare | $50 | Viable but may not work for all job locations |
| Transit pass | $90 | Best value for urban or suburban commuters |
| Used car | $350 | Includes estimated payment + basic insurance |
| New car | $550 | Includes payment + full-coverage insurance |

**Teaching note:** Many students will choose a car because it feels like the "adult" choice. Ask them: what would happen if the car needed a repair and they had no emergency fund?

---

### Station 3 — Groceries & Food

| Choice | Monthly Cost | Approximate Daily Food Budget |
|---|---|---|
| Meal prep at home | $200 | ~$6.67/day |
| Mix of home & out | $350 | ~$11.67/day |
| Mostly restaurants | $550 | ~$18.33/day |

**Teaching note:** The difference between meal prep and eating out is $350/year vs. $6,600/year — a $4,200 annual gap that most students do not initially perceive.

---

### Station 4 — Healthcare & Insurance

| Choice | Monthly Cost | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Employer plan | $150 | Lowest risk, most common for full-time workers |
| ACA marketplace | $250 | Higher cost, may have higher deductibles |
| No insurance | $0 | One ER visit average: $2,200–$3,000+ |

**Teaching note:** Students who choose no insurance often feel validated by the $0 cost — until you explain what an emergency appendectomy ($35,000+) or a broken arm ($7,500+) actually costs. This station creates one of the richest class discussions.

### Station 5 — Entertainment & Subscriptions

| Choice | Monthly Cost | Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Basic | $25 | $300/year |
| Standard | $75 | $900/year |
| Premium | $150 | $1,800/year |

**Teaching note:** Ask students to list every subscription they (or their household) actually pays for and add them up. Most will be shocked by the real number.

### Station 6 — Savings & Emergency Fund

| Choice | Cost for $2,005 net (lowest) | Cost for $2,901 net (highest) |
|---|---|---|
| No savings | $0 | $0 |
| 3% | $60.16 | $87.03 |
| 5% | $100.27 | $145.04 |
| 10% | $200.54 | $290.08 |

**Teaching note:** Savings is calculated as a percentage of each student's individual net pay — so this station produces a different dollar amount for every student. Discuss: what happens when a car breaks down or someone gets sick if there is no emergency fund?

### Station 7 — Student Loans & Debt

| Choice | Monthly Cost | Notes |

## Full Budget Scenarios

### Tightest Possible Budget (Childcare Worker, minimum spending)
- Net pay: $2,005.37
- Minimum choices total: $200 + $50 + $200 + $150 + $25 + $0 + $0 + $30 = **$655**
- Surplus: **$1,350.37** (but student loans = $0 may be unrealistic)

### Most Common "Broke" Scenario (Lower salary + one big choice)
- A Childcare Worker ($2,005 net) who chooses a studio apartment ($850) will spend **$850 on housing alone** — 42% of income — before paying for anything else.

### Realistic Middle Scenario (Warehouse Associate, balanced choices)
- Net pay: $2,536.56
- Shared housing ($650) + Transit ($90) + Mix food ($350) + Employer health ($150) + Standard entertainment ($75) + 5% savings ($127) + Community college ($150) + Average clothing ($80) = **$1,672**
- Surplus: **$864.56/month** — a reasonable outcome with disciplined choices

## Debrief Discussion Guide

### Opening (5 minutes)
- Quick raise of hands: How many finished with money left over? How many went over budget?
- What job did you get? How did that feel when you first saw it?

### Core Discussion Questions (15–20 minutes)

**On income and taxes:**
- What percentage of your gross pay went to taxes? Did that surprise you?
- What are Social Security and Medicare actually paying for?
- Some people feel taxes are too high; others feel they fund important things. What do you think, based on what you know now?

**On spending decisions:**
- Who chose to live alone? Who chose to live with parents? What made that feel right or wrong?
- How many people chose no health insurance? Now — what would happen if they broke an arm at work?
- Did anyone manage to save 10% of their income? What did they give up to do it?

**On the big picture:**
- What was the biggest difference between students who ended up with a surplus vs. a deficit?
- Is it possible to live well on a $29,000 salary? What would that actually require?
- Does a higher salary automatically mean a better life? Why or why not?

### Closing (5 minutes)
- What is one thing you will do differently because of this simulation?
- What is something this simulation could NOT show you about real financial life?

## Reflection Question Scoring Guide

| Question | What to Look For in Strong Responses |
|---|---|
| 1. Surprised by results? | Specific numbers cited; genuine reflection rather than generic answers |
| 2. Taxes fair or unfair? | Opinion supported by reasoning; mentions what taxes fund |
| 3. Largest expense analysis | Identifies the choice, names the alternative, explains the real-life consequence |
| 4. Would change one decision | Specific reasoning tied to budget outcome |
| 5. Higher salary comparison | Uses specific salary data; quantifies the difference |
| 6. Education & financial stability | Connects education path to income; mentions loan debt station |

## Extension Activities

### Extension 1 — The Second Run
Have students run the simulation a second time and intentionally try to maximize their monthly surplus. Then discuss: what quality-of-life trade-offs did they make? Is maximizing savings always the right goal?

### Extension 2 — The Budget Comparison Essay
Students who were assigned different salaries write a 1-page comparison: "What would my monthly budget look like if I had earned [the other salary]?" This builds mathematical literacy and empathy.

### Extension 3 — The Career Research Project
Students research what education, training, or certifications are required to earn a salary 20–30% above their simulation salary. They present a realistic pathway from their simulation job to a higher-earning position.

### Extension 4 — The Real Rent Check
Using Zillow, Apartments.com, or a local listing site, students find an actual apartment available in your area. They compare the real rent to the simulation options and calculate what percentage of their simulation salary it would consume.

### Extension 5 — The Life Event Card
Create a set of printed "Life Event" cards to add after Phase 3. Students draw one before seeing their report:
- "Your car needs $800 in repairs. Deduct this from your remaining balance."
- "You were sick for 3 days and missed work. Lose $350 in wages."
- "Your health insurance covered a $4,000 procedure. You owe $0." (reward for choosing coverage)
- "You had no health insurance. Emergency room visit: deduct $2,500."
- "You saved 10% — your emergency fund covered it. No deduction."

### Extension 6 — The Salary Negotiation Role Play
Students research what the same job pays in three different U.S. cities (e.g., Philadelphia, rural Pennsylvania, New York City) and compare salaries against the local cost of housing. Discuss: is a higher salary always better?
